Experience With UI and UX Week 3

The Norman Door I think this theory is a great way to introduce how user experience and user interface work in terms of any product really be it a door, a game, a website, and even clothing! It is important that the design of a product is not arbitrary and should always be clear as to what they do, when, and how, if a product fails to communicate that then it really is the designer’s fault and not yours as the user! This broadened my understanding of how important it is to remember as a designer and creator that what is in my head no one else can see or understand so I have to communicate clearly what my product or website is about and how it works, for example, if I have a button in my website, as per norm and learned convention the button should respond with whatever result I set it to. If the button does not interact in any way and it is just there for decoration, I have already failed as a designer and to please my client. Speaking of conventions, when I first engaged with Steven Mailloux reading about conventions and context I did not quite get how it was linked with UI and UX until some thorough thinking as to what exactly the text is about, Mailloux basically says that convention is a tradition passed down generation to generation or from academics to academics, these conventions are practiced because it is what people are used to and how we do things till today and that breaking these conventions could break a cycle and cause havoc, because now for example if a button does not have a function anymore then what does it do? And how will users get used to this invention? In the same breath, he does acknowledge that context can change, like the time and evolution of technology but the change of convention should be adjustable and understandable to all, like the book/paper hypermedia by Maria Fischer and Andrea Muller, these have been evolved from paper to digital screen, same convention but different context. Good UX and UI are when there is an interaction between the product and the user. We are provided with four definitions as to what interactivity is, Aaron Smuts says something is interactive if it is responsive if a user clicks on X at the top corner of a webpage the X should respond by taking the user to the homepage of the close page. Lev Monovish says that interactivity should not be random, if a user clicks on X it should not then take us to the next page or open a new website. Espen Aarseth and Gonzalo Frasca simply say that interactivity should be effortless/ easy.

Experience With Wireframes and UI Week 4

It is every web designer’s dream to create the coolest and most talked about website by having all cool animation effects and unending pop-ups, but it is all for nothing if the user does not know the purpose of your website or even knows how to use the interface. This week’s reading by UXPIN about the basic principles of user interface design made me realize the importance of planning before instilling in code, this planning process is basically drawing a blueprint on paper on how your website will look and work, it is also known as a wireframe. The wireframe will basically give you a guideline as to how the layout will look like and how it will work. This helps ensure that the design will be organized, intuitive and easy to use. By creating wireframes, designers can identify potential issues with the design early in the process. This saves time and money in the long run by preventing design flaws and usability problems that may arise later in the development process. Your design intention should speak for itself and be easy to understand, if your page needs the user to scroll up and down make sure you have something like a scroll bar or even signs that indicate up-and-down movement. Overall, wireframes are an essential part of the UI/UX design process. They help designers create a well-organized, user-friendly interface that meets the needs of both the user and the business. !

Changes And Updates On The Website Week 7

Building a website is no easy task, and as a passionate web developer, I recently underwent the exhilarating experience of creating my own online platform. However, the marks and feedback I received for my website left me with some valuable lessons. In this blog post, I would like to share my journey and the insightful feedback I received regarding three crucial aspects: semantic markups, headers, and image performance.
Semantic Markup: One of the main critiques I received was related to the lack of proper semantic markups on my website. It became evident that I hadn't utilized HTML tags effectively to convey the meaning and structure of my content. By not using tags like header, nav, section, and footer appropriately, I missed out on providing clear cues to search engines and assistive technologies.
Headers and Microformats: Another area that required improvement was the implementation of headers and microformats. The feedback pointed out that I hadn't structured my content using hierarchical headers (H1, H2, etc.) effectively. This led to a lack of readability and clarity for both users and search engine crawlers. Additionally, I overlooked the significance of microformats, which provide additional context to certain elements like addresses, events, or reviews. Their absence hindered the website's overall accessibility and search engine optimization (SEO).
Image Performance: The feedback further highlighted the need to enhance the performance of the images on my website. Images played a significant role in my content, but their sizes were inconsistent, resulting in slower loading times. This negatively affected user experience and could potentially discourage visitors from staying on my website.
Receiving constructive feedback about my website's performance was a valuable experience that helped me recognize the areas where I fell short. The importance of using proper semantic markups, headers, and microformats cannot be overstated, as they greatly impact a website's accessibility, search engine visibility, and overall user experience. Additionally, optimizing image performance is crucial for faster load times and a seamless browsing experience. Armed with this knowledge, I'm now motivated to revisit my website and make the necessary improvements, ensuring that my future online endeavors will be better equipped to meet the expectations of both users and search engines alike.

Changes That Were Implemented Week 8

After receiving valuable feedback regarding the readability of my blog page, I decided to take action and make significant changes to enhance the overall user experience. Recognizing the importance of easy-to-read content, I revamped the layout to address the previous challenge where the text expanded into a long, thin block, making it difficult for readers to navigate. In this blog post, I am excited to share the improvements I have made to ensure a seamless and enjoyable reading experience for my audience.
To address the readability issue, I made the decision to introduce a new feature: a dedicated page for each blog post. Now, when users click on a blog title, they are redirected to a separate page that provides a more spacious and user-friendly environment for reading the content. This layout change allows readers to fully immerse themselves in the text without losing track due to the elongated structure of the previous design.
By implementing this new design, I have made significant strides in improving the readability of my blog. Readers can now focus on the content without any distractions, enhancing their comprehension and engagement. Additionally, the separate blog pages enable better organization and navigation, making it easier for users to find specific articles and reference them in the future.
In the design section of my website, I received feedback highlighting two key issues: oversized images and difficulties in understanding wireframes, along with the tedious nature of scrolling to find relevant headings in my notes. To address these concerns, I implemented a revamped design approach.
Firstly, I made the images scrollable and neatly arranged, ensuring they were visually appealing without overwhelming the user. This allowed for a more seamless browsing experience, as users could now explore the images without any hindrances.
Secondly, I introduced a user-friendly navigation system by incorporating links for different design topics. This new feature allows users to easily select the specific topic they wish to read about, eliminating the need for excessive scrolling and ensuring quick access to relevant information.
By improving the image presentation and implementing a convenient navigation system, I aimed to enhance user understanding and streamline the reading experience. These changes empower users to explore design concepts effortlessly, while ensuring that the content is organized and easily accessible, leading to a more enjoyable and informative journey through the design section of my website.
